What do you expect from me as a judge?
For the randomly selected judges, please read the next section.

First and foremost, you are NOT judging a national competition. You are judging an ETDS. This means that people are not used to dancing with each other. Partnering and musicality is of a much higher difficulty than just basic technique. Does the couple take a big risk to thrill the crowd, but in return slightly lower the technical standard? Does the couple have a large height difference, but do they still manage to show partnering even though the frame is not amazing? Those should be rewarded rather than frowned upon. Of course, technique still plays a large part in judging, but don't let it overtake your judgement.

On top of that, the ETDS is about fun. Both in the dancer and in the adjudicator. Unlike normal competitions, judges are allowed and expect to smile and interact with the dancers and even each other if the situation permits.
What do you expect from me as a randomly selected judge?
Your job is to not look at technique. Your job instead is to identify the best couple in terms of choreography, musicality, audience interaction, couple interaction, etc.

Do you find this difficult? Think of the following things when you're judging;
  • Are they doing more than just basics?
  • Do they dance not just on rhythm, but really on the music?
  • Does their dancing make you feel something?
  • Does the crowd go wild in multiple places when they go by (to avoid just hearing their own team)?


Do understand however, that you have about 10s per couple at most to judge each couple. That is not a lot of time!

How to use the system
The system is fairly straight forward to use. While you will hear the summary during your briefing, here are some quick pointers:
  • At the top of the screen you will see:
    • the number of (or range of) crosses you need to give,
    • the number of heats,
    • the name of the current dance,
    • the number of crosses you have given,
    • and the number of couples you have noted.

  • The color of the top bar indicates the following:
    • Red: you have crossed too few or too many couples
    • Green: you have scored the right amount of couples
    • Blue: you have scored the right amount of couples but you still have some couples noted
    You always try to aim for green or blue.

  • To cross a couple you tap the empty box below their number
  • To un-cross a couple you tap the crossed box below their number
  • To note/mark a couple for your own convenience you tap their number. The note is indicated by a number of dots.
  • A couple can have 1, 2, or 3 dots/notes. You can use this, for example, to distinguish couples you have seen, may want to cross, or are sure you do not want to cross.
  • In case the system is slow or fails to respond, please refresh the page or tap the top bar.
  • In case the system does not respond even after a refresh, please DO NOT keep refreshing, but report at the tournament desk. Meanwhile, please finish the marking of that heat in a note on your phone or with pen and paper.
  • In the finals, you score couples by tapping the placing next to their number.
What happens when a couple misses their heat?
If a couple misses their heat, they get another chance by dancing in the last heat. Their number will be moved by the tournament office to that heat, so no intervention needs to be done on your part.
